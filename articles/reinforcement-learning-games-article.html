From Retrieval to Results: Bridging AI and Business with RAG on MS MARCO

By Hossein Khodadadi

As engineers and data scientists, we often focus deeply on model development and evaluation—sometimes at the expense of understanding how to communicate our work effectively to non-technical stakeholders. This realization hit home during my participation in the Artificial Intelligence and Data Science Prototype (ADSP) course at Politecnico di Torino, developed in collaboration with the LINKS Foundation.

This program not only deepened our technical skills but also helped us translate research into real-world, business-relevant outcomes. One of the key achievements of our team was enhancing a Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) system on the MS MARCO dataset, resulting in a 7.1% improvement in the Mean Reciprocal Rank (MRR) metric.

What is Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG)?

RAG is a hybrid AI architecture that combines two steps:

  • Retrieval: fetch relevant documents from a knowledge base.
  • Generation: use a language model to generate responses based on the retrieved documents.

This architecture offers more grounded and factual outputs than standalone language models.

What is MS MARCO?

MS MARCO (Microsoft MAchine Reading COmprehension) is a large-scale dataset for training and evaluating machine reading comprehension and retrieval models. It contains real user queries with human-generated answers, making it ideal for building question-answering systems.

Project Result

We fine-tuned both the retrieval and generation components of our RAG system. Using the MS MARCO dataset, we achieved a 7.1% improvement in MRR—a key evaluation metric that measures how highly the correct answer appears in the list of results. The improvement demonstrates enhanced precision and relevance of the generated content.

Business & Communication Insights

While the technical result was valuable, the course taught us how to better manage and present our work in business environments. Here are some tools and concepts that made the biggest impact:

  • Stakeholder Map: A diagram showing all parties involved in or affected by the project. Helps guide communication strategy.
  • Persona: A fictional user profile used to design human-centered solutions tailored to user needs.
  • Customer Journey Map: A visual overview of the customer’s experience across various touchpoints with the system.
  • GANTT Chart: A project timeline view showing tasks, durations, and dependencies over time.
  • Work Breakdown Structure (WBS): A hierarchical decomposition of project deliverables and tasks.
  • Deliverable: A tangible output (like a presentation or model) expected at a certain stage of the project.

Presentation Tip That Changed My Perspective

As engineers, we often build our presentations like research papers—starting with problem definitions and ending with results. However, the most valuable business insight we gained was this:

"Your punchline—the core result—should come first."

Business stakeholders want impact early. Lead with your improvement, then explain how you achieved it.

Final Reflection

This project taught me that technical excellence is only half the story. The other half lies in communication, user-centric thinking, and project management. The ADSP course and collaboration with LINKS Foundation helped me grow not just as a machine learning engineer—but also as a product-minded professional ready to deliver value in real-world contexts.
